One Step Forward Into A Billion. One Step Back For Black Retailers...

Monday, October 25, 2021

Sneakers have become a booming business in the retail industry. From the pricing on some of retail's most iconic designs rising every year to unofficial retailers (a.k.a. resellers) selling high-priced shoes for much higher than the original retail price. However, there is another issue that has been made aware of in recent updates. While much of the hype for sneakers like Air Jordans originated from black culture, much of the revenue seems to be going the opposite direction, away from black retail businesses. CEO and President of Nike, John Donahoe, released a report in March with the following statement: “Our brand would not be what it is today without the powerful contributions of Black athletes and Black culture.”

We have seen sneakers impact black culture as far back as the 1980s, especially in hip hop. Sneakers have revolutionized design for artists from Run DMC's 1986 song, "My Adidas" to the emergence of the Michael Jordan's Air Jordan shoe line, which is still in high demand all the way in 2021. Another collaboration comes from Nike's Air Max Griffey 1, the first signature shoe of baseball legend, Ken Griffey Jr in 1996. One of the biggest collaborations we are seeing today is Nike's and Jordan's collaboration with hip hop artist, Travis Scott, releasing the Travis Scott Air Max 1's and the Travis Scott Air Jordan 1's.

Black culture has been a driving force in incorporating sneakers into different areas of fashion as well as other areas in other industries. For many businesses, it is unfortunate than much of the revenue and profit is not making it back to the people of black culture who pushed and/or continue to push this influence in today's society.
