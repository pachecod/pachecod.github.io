Since 1991, the United States military has been engaged in over 200 countries, with a peak of over 426,000 troop deployments in 2007. But where they have been engaged has differed wildly by region. This story gives a bird's-eye view of the major deployments across five regions around the world.

Africa

Analysis by Dan Papillo, Liam Robertson, Ryan Randone and Sumner Baines.

The reason for heightened US presence is that the Headquarters of AFRICOM resides in Djibouti. The US owns a military base there paying up to 63 million a year in rent to the Djibouti Government. Several other governments also retain military base camps. China and Russia currently both hold leases for military camps in Djibouti. Obama signed a 20 year lease to maintain the United States Military presence in AFRICOM.

Asia

Analysis by Anna McCusker, Caden McDermott and Cam Croook.

After 2005, the data shows a pretty consistent increasing trend of U.S. troops stationed in Asia. This could be attributed to Operation Enduring Freedom, which was part of the U.S' Global War on Terror following the events of 9/11. The U.S targeted the leaders of various jihadist groups that were operating in the country. Other locations that feature higher numbers of U.S troop presence are areas that are still potentially dangerous, like Korea, or nations that we have an amicable relationship with like Japan. Also, some of the locations with more U.S troops are crucial strategic chokepoints on the South China sea, as the U.S struggles for hegemony with China.

Europe

Analysis by Stefanie Mitchell and Ben Bascuk.

After the end of the Cold War, the amount of U.S. troops stationed across West Europe dropped significantly. Britain, France, and West Germany had stationed tens-hundreds of thousands of troops every year before the 1990s. The post Cold War period saw U.S. personnel reductions of 50 percent or more in countries such as Portugal, Iceland, Greece, and the Netherlands.

The Middle East

Analysis by Isabella Galan, Siggy Bell and Marissa Cogan.

This data highlighted the U.S. presence in the middle east, spiking in 2003 originally, then higher in 2007. The spike was because of the 9/11 attacks and the Global War on Terror. It seems the U.S. pulled out the majority starting in 2011. As of 2020, the number was lower than it was in 2001.
