Since 1991, the United States military has been engaged in over 200 countries, with a peak of 800,000. But where they have been engaged has been wildly different by region. This story gives a bird's-eye view of the major deployments across five regions. See the code for this page on its Github repository.

Africa

By Eric McCormick and Jacob Kaye

Major decrease after 1993 due to the end of apartheid and an Ethiopian civil war. The second increase in the mid 2010’s may be because of conflicts in the Central African Republic. Africa consistently has ethnic conflicts and other disputes that lead to troops being stationed in the continent. On top of actual military conflict, outbreaks and public health emergencies such as Ebola also led to increased numbers of troops in Africa.

Asia

In Asia, USA had most troops deployed in Japan and Korea. Due to major recent tensions with the country, the numbers in China are not significant enough to appear on the chart. US presence in Japan peaked at over 55,000 troops in 2016, but fell by almost 20,000 by 2017.

Latin America

In Panama, the US had well over 10,000 troops in the early 1990s. But as the decade wore on, the number decreased to almost zero by the turn into 2000. The reasoning is the Panama Canal Treaty of 1977 signed by the US Government and Panama leaders in the Carter administration that gave up American control of the canal and mandated all US forces would withdraw from the territory by the end of 1999.

Cuba had close to 2,500 US troops stationed there in 1991, in 2020 there were 730 troops stationed there. Cuba has had better relations with the U.S. since 1991. Similar to Cuba, Honduras had 1000 U.S. troops stationed there in 1991 and in 2019 there were only 370 U.S. troops stationed there.

In Haiti, the US had very few troops in the area at the start of the 90s before dramatically spiking to well over 15,000 in 1994 before shrinking back down to the early 90s numbers throughout the 21st century. The reason is because of a US led military intervention as part of “Operation Uphold Democracy.” The troop invasion occured after a 1991 Haitian coup that overthrew elected President Jean-Bertrand Aristide. The goal of the mission (which was approved by the UN) was to restore Aristide to his former power and remove the military general who refused to allow democratic elections after taking the country over. It was successful after a few months, which led to the withdrawal of US troops to be replaced by a UN Mission. This caused the numbers to drop back.

In Suriname, the US had almost no troops in the area before spiking to over 150 troops around 1995, before dropping back down to almost zero by the end of the decade, and staying that way for the 21st century. The spike occurred as part of a temporary agreement between the US and Suriname governments to stage training in that area for around 2 years before dropping down to almost no troops. The training was also meant to strengthen bonds between the two nations.

The Middle East

By Luke Backman, Andrew Selover and Lilly Evans

In 2003, the United States increased its military presence in Iraq from 0 troops to 136,000 troops. This commenced The Iraq War. US troops invaded Iraq in March of 2003 on a vow to destroy all weapons of mass destruction and put an end to the rule of Saddam Hussein. The United States maintained a major troop presence despite President Bush’s declaration that major combat ended as of May 2003. The troop presence steadily decreased beginning in 2008 because Barack Obama was elected and decided to back out the troops.

In 2011 over 10,000 United States troops were located and based in Kyrgyzstan. This had been the case for years as this was an area that the U.S. had set up a base in. In 2014 the U.S. vacated the base having only 121 troops located in Kyrgyzstan. As years go on the number of troops has dwindled even more as in recent years troops located there have been in the single digits.
